# Digilog — Vision Statement

*Written April 2026, during the first working session.*

---

## What Digilog is

Digilog is a free, open, physical audio format.

It encodes music into printed dots — on stickers, cards, posters, walls, and discs.
Scan it with any camera. Hear the music. No internet. No platform. No permission needed.

---

## The two forms

Digilog exists in two physical forms, designed for different moments:

### Form 1 — The Flat Code (stickers, cards, posters)

A grid of colored dots printed on any flat surface.

A sticker on a guitar case. A business card handed at a show.
A poster wheatpasted on a wall. A tag on a piece of art.

Scan it once. Hear the song.

This is music as graffiti. Music as gift. Music as artifact.
An indie artist can print 100 stickers for a few dollars and hand their song
to 100 strangers. No Spotify. No link. No algorithm. Just dots on paper.

Low resolution versions — small, cheap, fast — spread like seeds.
High resolution versions — large, dense, rich — reward the people who seek them out.

**The size of the print is the quality of the sound.**
A sticker sounds like a demo. A poster sounds like a record.
The physical object tells you what to expect.

### Form 2 — The Digilog Disc (the spinning record)

A circular printed disc that spins on a turntable.
A phone camera mounted above reads the dots as they pass.
The music plays continuously as the disc rotates.

This is vinyl — but printed. Open. Reproducible.

The disc has concentric data rings, like vinyl grooves:
- Outer ring — highest quality, densest dots, best camera needed
- Middle ring — mid quality, readable on any modern phone  
- Inner ring — core layer, always readable, survives everything

The needle drops on the outer ring for best quality.
A worn disc, a cheap camera, a scratched print — falls back to the inner ring.
You hear what the physical object can give you. Exactly like vinyl.

A 3D-printed phone rig mounts above the turntable at a fixed angle and distance.
The turntable spins. The camera reads. The music plays.
Anyone can print the disc. Anyone can build the rig. Anyone can write a reader.

---

## The two encoding modes

These two forms require two different encoding approaches:

### Mode 1 — Discrete (current, v0.1)

Hard-edged colored squares. 8 colors. 3 bits per dot.
Read the center of each dot. Fast, robust, forgiving.
Works on any camera, any printer, any surface.

Best for: stickers, cards, posters, walls — anything scanned once, quickly.

### Mode 2 — Gradient (future, v2.0)

Soft dots with controlled gradient transitions between neighbors.
The blend zone between two dots encodes additional data.
More capacity. More organic. Designed for motion.

Best for: the Digilog Disc — the gradient blur from spinning
actually helps reading. Motion is signal, not noise.
Also produces richer prints — closer to CRT phosphor glow than digital pixels.

## The spectrum of use

```
wheat paste poster    →  large, free, anonymous, low res
                         music as public art
                         
sticker               →  small, cheap, personal, shareable
                         music as gift
                         
business card         →  compact, printable at home
                         music as introduction
                         
postcard              →  mid quality, sendable
                         music as letter
                         
Digilog Disc          →  high quality, spinning, ritual
                         music as record
```

Every point on this spectrum is the same format.
The same `.dtw` file. The same open spec. The same free decoder.
Just different physical expressions of the same idea.

---

## The open promise

Digilog is and will always be free.

Free to encode. Free to decode. Free to print. Free to share.
Free to build on. Free to remix. Free to spin.

No company owns the dots.
No platform controls the music.
No algorithm decides who hears what.

You print it. You hand it. They scan it. They hear it.

That's all.

---

## Why this matters

The music industry has spent 30 years building walls between artists and listeners.
Streaming platforms take 70%. Algorithms decide who gets heard.
A song needs a URL to exist. A URL needs a server. A server needs a company.

Digilog needs none of that.

A song encoded in Digilog exists as long as the paper it's printed on exists.
It plays without internet. Without permission. Without a middleman.

An indie artist with a printer and a dream can hand their music
to anyone, anywhere, forever.

*Scan the groove.*
